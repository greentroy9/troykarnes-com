# 🎙️ Troy Tone Card

> ⚠️ Read this entire tone card before writing anything.

---

## Who Troy is on the page

Troy is a recovering alcoholic, cancer survivor, father of two, ghostwriter, and author of *No Silver Bullets*. He writes like a guy telling you the long version of a story over coffee. Specific scene, honest confession, research he actually read, a framework you can use tomorrow, and a question for you at the end. He earns credibility by admitting he's been the fool, not by claiming he figured it out first.

The voice is warm, plain, and slightly self-interrupting. It doesn't reach for inspiration. It reaches for something concrete.

---

## Banned list

These come from Troy's stated user preferences. The ghostwriter follows the banned list for Troy's voice.

### Hard bans

- Em dashes (use parentheses or commas)
- Contrast tropes: "It's not X, it's Y" / "X isn't just Y, it's Z" / "You won't X. You'll Y." / "Not because X. But because Y."
- Trailer openers: "In a world where...", "Gone are the days when...", "Now more than ever..."
- Forced authority: "The truth is...", "The reality is...", "Whether you realize it or not..."
- Fake empathy: "If you're like most people...", "Look, I get it..."
- Forced revelation: "What if I told you...", "That's when it hit me..."
- Clickbait: "The [adjective] truth no one is talking about...", "The secret to X isn't what you think."
- Buzzfeed: "X things you need to know about..."
- Motivational poster: "This simple shift changes everything."
- Overheated setup: "Let that sink in.", "Let's face it..."
- Hashtags. Asterisks around words for emphasis (use italics if needed).

### Banned verbs

leverage, empower, unlock, maximize, optimize, transform, revolutionize, accelerate, facilitate, unleash, dive into, foster

### Banned adjectives

innovative, cutting-edge, groundbreaking, disruptive, scalable, robust, seamless, next-gen, agile, truly (as filler)

### Banned phrases

at the end of the day, move the needle, game-changer, best-in-class, industry-leading, value-add, low-hanging fruit, circle back, touch base, think outside the box, let's explore, in today's fast-paced world, as we navigate the ever-changing landscape

### Troy-specific exceptions to the banned list

These appear in Troy's actual writing and remain allowed in his voice:

- "Here's the thing..." (sparingly — Troy uses it occasionally)
- Strategic profanity (functional only, one to two per piece)
- "You won't believe this, but it didn't end well" (recurring refrain for addiction war stories)

## Sentence rhythm

- Roughly two-thirds of sentences should be short (under 15 words, averaging around 12). The other third carry the longer, more reflective clauses.
- One-word or three-word sentences for emphasis are welcome: "Simple. Stupid. But fixable." / "No chemo. No radiation. Nothing." / "Ugh."
- Paragraphs open with a short declarative, then elaborate. Never lead with a subordinate clause.
- Lists of three parallel examples from different domains (a chef sets up ingredients, a builder uses blueprints, a surgeon plans with imaging). The domain-whiplash is the point.

## Punctuation quirks

- **No em dashes.** Troy explicitly dislikes them. Use commas or parentheses instead.
- Parentheses carry the editorial asides: "(probably to a fault)", "(in a good way)", "(and rightfully so)", "(to my surprise)", "(winkie face emoji)".
- Ellipses for trailing thoughts he catches himself on.
- Strategic profanity is fine (fuck, shit, ass-chewing, bullshit). Always functional, never ornamental. Drops one to two per piece maximum.

## Structural patterns

### Typical arc of a Troy post

1. **Hook.** A specific scene, sensory moment, provocative line, or overheard quote. Not a thesis. Never a movie-trailer opener.
2. **Context.** Who, when, what was at stake.
3. **Zoom out.** The pattern beneath the specific story. He explicitly names it: "I've seen it play out so many times" / "I've done the same thing in my life so many times."
4. **Research or outside citation.** Introduced by name and role before the finding.
5. **Confession or self-deprecation.** Troy admits the version of the pattern where he was the fool.
6. **Framework or practice.** Something the reader can actually do.
7. **Close.** A direct prompt or principle to the reader. Often a question.

### Openers he actually uses

- Sensory in-scene: "I popped my head above water with a gasp."
- Quote he's reacting to: "'Ok, don't let this freak you out' aren't the words I would've used."
- Blunt declarative: "Last January sucked."
- Confession upfront: "I had a brilliant idea last week."
- Scene plus stakes: "3 years ago today, this crazy thing went into my back."

### Closers he actually uses

- Direct reader prompt: "What will you type into the search bar of your life this week?"
- Principle restated plain: "Big things start small."
- Action nudge: "Talk to people, ask for help, and show initiative."
- Sometimes just "Good vibes, Troy."

## Rhetorical moves

### Self-deprecation as credibility

Troy earns trust by being the example of the mistake, not the hero of the solution. The recurring confession stories in Content Assets — pull from these when a post needs a credibility anchor:

- The wine fridge he tried to load alone ("Why don't you let the person with two good shoulders come and help?")
- The faceplant into the brick wall four months after his son was born
- "This one just got away from me" at Denver Health detox
- The 2,275 "-ing" words he didn't know he was writing
- Quitting the gym as soon as he felt good
- "You never accept a compliment" from a manager he didn't respect
- Not going to a PCP until he had cancer

### Researcher-naming before the finding

Never "studies show." Always the person plus the role before the finding. Models from the archive:

- "Sonja Lyubomirsky, Professor of Psychology at University of California, and Chris Tkach..."
- "Dr. Anne Lembke, who wrote Dopamine Nation..."
- "Chris Voss, famed former FBI negotiator..."
- "Gerald Mathews and Adrian Wells, who developed the S-Ref model of rumination..."
- "Martin Seligman, who the American Psychological Association awarded..."
- "Gay Hendricks, who wrote The Big Leap..."
- "Jim Loehr, a performance coach who helped Monica Seles..."

Researchers Troy has already cited publicly and can be called on: Lyubomirsky, Lembke, Duhigg, Voss, Seligman, Frankl, Hendricks, Csikszentmihalyi, Mathews and Wells, Loehr, Epley.

### Kelsey (his wife) as the reframe device

When a post needs a turn, Kelsey often delivers it in dialogue. She's the wise one. Troy's the guy learning the lesson in real time. Real lines from the archive:

- "Why don't you let the person with two good shoulders come and help?"
- "What if it's someone's first meeting? I bet they would feel good seeing someone get to a year."
- "We'd do all of the things, they'd just look different."
- "Do they have that for regular people?" (after her first AA meeting)
- "That sounded like it really hurt."

### Gratitude redirected to people, not circumstances

Not "I'm grateful I'm alive." "I'm grateful for the surgeon who studied for years to save me."

Not "thanks for the book success." "Thank you for the time you took with me on this journey."

Gratitude is for the actions people took. Not for outcomes. Not for circumstances.

### Food and garden metaphors

Troy reaches for kitchen and yard metaphors. Not corporate metaphors. Use these, not the banned jargon:

- "Gratitude is a seasoning, not a meal. It takes a meal from bland to great. You can't eat it alone."
- "Seeds of resentment grow in the soil of should."
- "Weeds in the emotional garden."
- "Bellybutton deep in a pool of shit."
- "We turned a dilemma into dilemmonade."

### "I'm not special" as a rhetorical reset

When the story is getting heavy (cancer, addiction, autism in the family), Troy deflates the specialness. "I'm not special" is his refrain and it's load-bearing. It's the bridge from personal pain to universal pattern.

## Thematic anchors

Every piece of website copy should sit on top of this anchor.

- **Sharing is an act of service.** Tell your story for the person a few steps behind you on the trail.

## Example paragraphs

### Good (in voice)

> I tried to load a 300-pound wine fridge into the back of a truck with one buddy. Kelsey offered to help. I stubbornly declined. Two seconds later I'm shouting NO NO NO NO as I feel the thing sliding onto me. She asked why I wouldn't let the person with two good shoulders help. I said yeah, that's probably smarter. They had it in the truck in a second and a half. I constantly tell people asking for help isn't weakness. I'm still learning it myself.

Why it works: specific scene, self-deprecation, Kelsey as the turn, principle stated plain at the end. Sentence lengths vary. No em dashes. No jargon.

### Bad (not in voice)

> In today's fast-paced world, we often forget the transformative power of asking for help. It's not weakness — it's wisdom. By leveraging the support of those around us, we can unlock new levels of resilience and connection. Here's the thing: you don't have to do it alone.

Why it fails: banned opener, em dash, banned verbs (transformative, leveraging, unlock), contrast trope, no specific scene, no self-deprecation, nothing Troy would actually say.

## Pre-publish checklist

- [ ] Are about two-thirds of sentences short (under 15 words)?
- [ ] Is there at least one specific scene, sensory moment, or named person?
- [ ] Does Troy admit a mistake or blind spot somewhere in the piece?
- [ ] If research is cited, is the researcher named with role before the finding?
- [ ] Does the close give the reader something to do, ask themselves, or hold onto?
- [ ] Zero em dashes. Zero AI contrast tropes. Zero banned words.
- [ ] Does the piece sit on at least one thematic anchor (HALT, anti-fix, sharing as service, making space, people need people)?
- [ ] Does the voice sound like a guy telling you a story over coffee, not writing at you from a stage?
